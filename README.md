# The Hidden Costs of Climate Change Tracking: How Climate Velocity and Movement Energetics Shape Connectivity Across European Protected Areas

### Study overview

Protected areas (PAs) remain central to biodiversity conservation, but climate change is increasingly undermining their long-term ability to safeguard the species and conditions they were established to protect. As climates shift, many PAs may no longer retain suitable local conditions, making persistence dependent not only on protection in place, but also on whether species can move through the landscape to reach more climatically stable areas. 

This study develops a **climate-informed connectivity framework** for Europe that links four key elements: **climate velocity**, **protected-area climatic residence time**, **PA size**, and **functional connectivity based on energetics-informed resistance surfaces**. The aim is to identify where climate-tracking movement is realistically feasible under both climatic and energetic constraints, and where it is likely to fail despite apparent structural connectivity. 

### Why this matters

Many connectivity assessments assume that species can move wherever habitat appears continuous. This can overestimate real climate-tracking capacity because it ignores the physiological and energetic difficulty of movement across terrain, land-use mosaics, and human-modified landscapes. In practice, corridors that look connected on a map may still be functionally inaccessible. This study addresses that gap by combining climate exposure and climatic persistence with a mechanistic representation of movement cost. 

### What this study does

The workflow integrates climate exposure and landscape permeability across Europe to evaluate whether climate-exposed PAs are connected to areas where suitable climates are more likely to persist.

It does this in four main steps:

1. **Quantifying climate exposure and resilience**
   Climate velocity is calculated from high-resolution temperature projections to estimate how quickly climatic conditions shift across the landscape. These values are summarized within terrestrial protected areas, and **climatic residence time** is estimated as the expected duration a PA retains its current climatic conditions. Residence time depends on both internal climate velocity and PA size, meaning that larger PAs can provide greater internal climatic buffering than smaller ones.  

2. **Estimating energetic movement costs**
   The study builds an **energy landscape** from terrain slope using a normalized movement-energetics model. This produces a body-mass-independent measure of the relative energetic difficulty of movement across terrain at continental scale. 

3. **Building resistance surfaces from habitat suitability and barriers**
   Species distribution models are used to estimate habitat suitability for focal mammal taxa, including predators and herbivores with contrasting ecological requirements. Suitability is transformed into resistance, and major barriers such as rivers, lakes, glaciers, motorways, and border barriers are explicitly added. This is then combined with the energetics layer to create a **composite resistance surface** representing both structural and physiological constraints on movement. 

4. **Mapping functional connectivity**
   Connectivity is modelled using **Omniscape**, an omnidirectional circuit-theory approach that estimates movement potential continuously across the landscape rather than only between predefined nodes. Outputs are summarized for two broad archetypes, **herbivores** and **carnivores**, and linked to climatic resilience within and around protected areas. 

### Main findings

#### 1. Climate exposure is widespread across Europe’s protected-area network

Nearly half of terrestrial protected areas in Europe (**46.3%**) are classified as **climate-exposed**, with short climatic residence times indicating rapid turnover of local climatic conditions. These exposed PAs are concentrated mainly in **lowland biogeographic regions**, including the Continental, Pannonian, Atlantic, and Mediterranean zones. 

#### 2. Climatically resilient PAs cluster in mountain systems

About **23.8%** of protected areas are classified as **climate-resilient**, with longer residence times and greater potential to function as long-term climatic refugia. These PAs are concentrated in major mountain systems such as the **Alps, Pyrenees, Carpathians, Cantabrian Mountains, Apennines, and Scandinavian highlands**, where steep climatic gradients reduce effective climate velocity. 

#### 3. PA size matters

The manuscript places stronger emphasis on the role of **protected-area size** than the earlier summary did. Small PAs are especially vulnerable because they provide limited internal climatic buffering and therefore shorter windows for in situ persistence. When short residence times coincide with small and fragmented reserves, species become more dependent on movement beyond PA boundaries on relatively short timescales. 

#### 4. Connectivity differs between herbivores and carnivores

A key result that differs from your earlier draft is that **herbivore connectivity is described as broader and more continuous across much of Europe**, including Fennoscandia, parts of Iberia, and several mountain and foothill systems. **Carnivore connectivity is more spatially restricted and channeled**, with much lower movement potential across large parts of western and central Europe. In both groups, topographically complex and less intensively modified landscapes support stronger connectivity, while flat, human-dominated lowlands act as major movement constraints. 

#### 5. Climatic resilience and connectivity often do not align

Overlaying connectivity with climatic resilience reveals strong geographic mismatches. Some regions contain PAs that are climatically stable but poorly connected, while others contain well-connected PAs that remain highly climate-exposed. These mismatches are especially important because they show that neither climatic refugia nor connectivity alone is enough: long-term persistence depends on their co-occurrence.  

### Core message

The central conclusion is that the effectiveness of Europe’s protected-area network under climate change depends on more than the location of future refugia. It also depends on **PA size** and on whether the surrounding landscape is **energetically permeable enough to support redistribution**. Climate adaptation is therefore not simply a matter of identifying connected habitat, but of identifying **feasible movement pathways** under shifting climates. 

### Conservation relevance

This framework provides a spatially explicit basis for identifying where restoration and conservation action could most improve climate-tracking potential across Europe. It is especially relevant for planning around the **Trans-European Nature Network**, national restoration strategies, and broader biodiversity policy focused on connectivity and climate adaptation. Rather than maximizing connectivity everywhere, the findings support prioritizing the places where improving permeability would most strengthen movement from highly exposed protected areas toward more stable parts of the network.
